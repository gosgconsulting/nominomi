Why DHA matters for your child's brain development

DHA (docosahexaenoic acid) is an Omega-3 fatty acid that makes up a major structural part of the brain and the retina. During the first years of life, a child's brain grows faster than at any other stage — and DHA is one of the building blocks that supports this remarkable growth.

What does DHA actually do?

Research links adequate DHA intake to better focus, sharper memory, healthier vision, and even improved mood regulation in young children. Because the body produces only small amounts of DHA on its own, most of what your child needs has to come from food or supplementation.

How much DHA do kids need?

Health authorities generally recommend that children aged 1–8 get around 100–250 mg of DHA per day. The exact amount varies by age, but the message is consistent: a regular, reliable source is more important than the occasional big dose.

Good sources of DHA

  • Oily fish such as salmon, sardines and mackerel
  • DHA-fortified eggs and dairy
  • Algal oil for vegetarian families
  • Nutritionally enhanced juices like NOMI NOMI
