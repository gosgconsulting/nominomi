Hidden sugars in kids' drinks — and how to avoid them

That cheerful, fruit-themed bottle in the supermarket aisle isn't always as innocent as it looks. Many drinks marketed to children contain large amounts of added sugar — or, increasingly, artificial sweeteners that come with their own concerns.

Why hidden sugar is a problem

Excess sugar in early childhood is linked to tooth decay, weight gain, energy crashes and even fussy eating behaviour, because sweet drinks dull the appetite for real meals. The World Health Organization recommends keeping added sugar below 10% of daily calories, and ideally under 5% for children.

The artificial-sweetener trap

"Zero sugar" or "no sugar added" sounds healthy, but many products simply swap real sugar for sucralose, aspartame or stevia. Recent research suggests these sweeteners may disrupt gut bacteria and confuse hunger signals — neither of which is ideal for a developing little body.

How to read a children's drink label

  1. Check the ingredients list. Sugar hides under names like glucose syrup, fructose, dextrose, maltose and concentrated fruit juice.
  2. Scan for sweeteners. Look for sucralose, aspartame, acesulfame K, stevia, sorbitol or xylitol.
  3. Compare per 100 ml, not per serving. Servings can be misleadingly small.
  4. Aim for under 5 g of sugar per 100 ml as a sensible target.

The better-choice checklist

  • No added sugar
  • No artificial sweeteners
  • Vitamins from real fruits and vegetables, not just synthetic blends
  • No artificial colours or preservatives
