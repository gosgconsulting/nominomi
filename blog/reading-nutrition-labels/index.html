A quick parent's guide to reading nutrition labels

Nutrition labels can feel like a wall of numbers, percentages and unfamiliar words. The good news? You only really need to scan five things to make a good decision for your child.

1. Start with the serving size

Every other number on the label is calculated per serving. A "small" pack might actually contain two or three servings, which doubles or triples everything else. Always check this first.

2. Sugar — both kinds

Look for "of which sugars" under total carbohydrates. As a rule of thumb for children's drinks, under 5 g of sugar per 100 ml is a good target. Anything over 10 g per 100 ml is essentially a soft drink.

3. Read the ingredients list

Ingredients are listed by weight — the first ones are the biggest part of the product. If sugar (or any of its many aliases) appears in the top three, it's a sweet treat, not a daily drink.

Watch for these sugar aliases: glucose syrup, fructose, dextrose, maltose, corn syrup, concentrated fruit juice, agave, honey.

4. Spot artificial additives

Common ones to flag for young children:

  • Sweeteners: sucralose, aspartame, acesulfame K, stevia
  • Colours: tartrazine (E102), sunset yellow (E110), allura red (E129)
  • Preservatives: sodium benzoate (E211), potassium sorbate (E202)

5. Check what it actually delivers

A good label tells you the positives, not just what to avoid: vitamins, minerals, fibre, real fruit or vegetable content, omega fatty acids. If a "healthy" drink doesn't add anything beneficial, it probably isn't.

The 30-second rule

If you've only got 30 seconds in the supermarket: glance at the serving size, then the sugar per 100 ml, then the first three ingredients. That's enough to filter out 90% of the bad options.
